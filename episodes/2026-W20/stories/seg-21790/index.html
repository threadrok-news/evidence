US consumer inflation rose to 3.8% in April, exceeding the Federal Reserve's target and reflecting broad price increases across energy, food, and core goods categories.

Full Narration

US inflation came in at 3.8% for April. That's up from the prior month and sits meaningfully above the Federal Reserve's 2% target. The number lands at a moment when tariff-driven cost pressures have been building across goods categories. The Hill flagged that the April figure reflects price increases in energy, food, and core goods simultaneously, which makes the composition of this reading different from earlier inflation spikes that were more narrowly concentrated. Here's the context: the Fed has held rates steady through the early part of this year. A 3.8% print doesn't force an immediate pivot, but it narrows the window for any rate cuts that markets were pricing in for late 2026.
